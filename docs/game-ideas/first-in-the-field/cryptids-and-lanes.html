Cryptids, Trophies & Lanes

Bringing real & not-so-real species into the game — and the structures that hold them
Companion to three-planes.md & first-in-the-field-cards.md · working draft

This document does three things: it deepens the trophy idea (named species as the prizes your engine is built to win), it lays out the cryptid / fraud system in full — including five ways the truth gets resolved and four different ways the whole thing can be wired into the game, each with concrete effects, actions, and resources — and it develops the two candidate new lanes, the Expedition and the Cabinet. It ends with a decision matrix so you can pick a direction with a pencil in hand.

1The pivot: species as trophies

The game currently feels shallow for one structural reason: the species — the entire point — are invisible. You farm generic Specimens, refine generic Findings, and lodge generic claims. There is nothing specific to want, so there is nothing to plan toward and no reason to feel attached to what you collect. The five fields (Botany, Entomology, Ornithology, Geology, the New Genus) are merely map locations, not distinct resources.

The fix that buys both theme and depth in a single move: make named species the concrete prizes the engine exists to win. A shared, face-up row of full-art Species cards becomes the objective layer. Your Field → Study → Hall chain is now for naming them first. This adds an objective/planning layer, a set-collection layer, and a real priority race (two players wanting the same species) — without disturbing the Specimens → Findings → Claims spine.

Why it deepens play
Trophies turn an abstract track-leading engine into a board of puzzles with deadlines. Every species is a recipe (what Findings, from which fields), a risk (can a rival scoop or refute it?), and a reward (an ongoing power plus end-game prestige). Strategy stops being "optimise my ratios" and becomes "which discoveries am I racing for, and who's racing me?"

2How trophies work (the species card)

A Species card is a small object with five working parts. Four are public; on cryptids a fifth is hidden (see §3).

Anatomy of a Species card
PartWhat it does
RarityCommon · Rare · Sensational · Dubious. Sets cost, reward, and whether it can be disputed.
TraitsKeywords (Winged, Aquatic, Venomous, Iridescent, Gigantic, Nocturnal, Anomalous, Legendary). The synergy hooks.
Name itThe recipe: a number of Findings, often typed to specific fields. This is what finally makes the five fields play differently.
When namedA permanent passive — so naming is power, not only points.
Scores / SetEnd-game Standing, plus which collection set it belongs to (Birds, Beasts, Flora, Monsters…).

Typed recipes make the fields matter

Right now a Finding is a Finding. Tie recipes to fields and each field becomes a distinct resource you must actually develop. A bird wants Ornithology Findings; a fossil wants Geology; the platypus, infamously, wants Findings from three different fields because nobody could agree what it was. Specialists race the easy single-field species; generalists unlock the anomalies no one else can touch.

Because traits are drafted into your collection over the game, each playthrough grows a different "build" — the cheart of replayable engine play. A run where Iridescent beetles flood the row plays nothing like a run dominated by Gigantic fossils.

Set collection & the trophy lifecycle

Beyond their individual rewards, named species feed sets scored at the Exhibition (e.g. "the complete order of British Birds," "a creature from every field," "three Monsters"). Sets are where the Cabinet lane earns its keep (§5). A trophy's life runs: drafted into the row → recipe assembled in the Study → named in the Hall (possibly contested) → displayed in the Cabinet for end-game prestige. Each stage is a different lane doing a different job — which is the argument that the lanes reinforce one another rather than just piling up.

3The cryptid & fraud system

Real and not-so-real beasts whose reality is variable — the Kraken is real this game, a sailor's yarn the next — resolved by bluff, by the whole table's judgement, or by weighing evidence against chance.

The shared foundation: variable reality

Every Cryptid pairs with a face-down Truth token (Genuine / Hoax), seeded at setup. Nothing on the card's face changes; only its hidden truth does. That single trick delivers replayability and genuine uncertainty at almost no rules cost. The design question each mechanism below answers is the same: who knows the truth, and how does the table learn it?

The "feed the kraken" texture you asked for
Contributions to a cryptid are added face-down and anonymously, so it is genuinely unclear who found it. A creature swells in the centre of the table as players quietly feed it — and when it finally resolves, the question of who over-committed is half the drama.

Five ways to resolve the truth

A · The Sealed Dossier — pure bluff

Only the discoverer peeks at the Truth token. They may publish it as real (staking reputation) or quietly sell it as a curiosity. Rivals decide whether to Challenge by reading the discoverer — and a sharp player publishes a known hoax with total confidence (the double-bluff). Challenge flips the token: Genuine and the challenger eats the loss; Hoax and the discoverer is ruined. Poker with reputations; intimate and tense.

B · The Verdict of the Society — social deduction

Nobody owns the truth. Players hold secret leanings (Believer / Skeptic) or private clue cards hinting real-or-fake, and the creature's official status is decided by a table vote at the Exhibition. You score for backing the side that wins and/or the side that proves true. The loud, accusatory flavour — a big tonal swing from the cold euro, so best as a variant/module, but the purest "whole table figures it out."

C · The Weight of Evidence — evidence vs. randomiser

The truth is not predetermined — players move it. Each Finding committed drops a token onto the Genuine or Doubt side (or into a shared bag). At resolution you draw weighted by what has been fed: more genuine evidence, better odds of confirmation. Players literally fund the truth. Clean, no hidden roles, slots straight into the existing engine.

D · The Kraken Pot — brinkmanship + hidden commitment

This is the literal "feed the kraken." One legendary beast sits centre-table as a communal pot. Players add sightings face-down and anonymously; the pot swells, and the bigger it grows the bigger the payoff if real — and the deeper the disgrace if exposed. At the trigger it resolves by weighted draw: Genuine → the largest contributor claims priority and glory; Hoax → the largest contributor is the most ruined, while small dabblers walk away clean. A chicken/brinkmanship dynamic layered over hidden commitment: feed it enough to win the prize, but not so much you become the patsy.

E · Schrödinger's Specimen — reality authored by play

Reality is decided by what players do: validate the creature with enough rigorous Findings before the clock and it is canonised real forever; let the season close unsupported, or let it be debunked, and it retroactively becomes a hoax that sinks everyone who backed it. A pure race-to-validate — the most euro-native option.

On the randomiser itself
Prefer an evidence bag over dice: players add Genuine/Doubt tokens as they commit Findings, and the reveal is a draw from the bag, so the evidence becomes the odds (uncertain but never arbitrary) and "drawing a conclusion from the evidence" is on-theme. Dice are the swingier, faster alternative; a stacked Truth-deck draw sits between the two. The standout pairing of everything you described is the Kraken Pot resolved by an evidence bag.

4Four ways to wire cryptids in

The five mechanisms above answer how the truth resolves. This section answers a different question: how deeply is the whole cryptid system wired into the game, and what does it add? Four models, from a detachable side-bet to a second economy. Each lists the new resources, actions, and effects it introduces.

LightA · The Side Show

A self-contained high-stakes mini-game humming beside the core. Ignore it entirely and you still have a complete game.

RoleAn optional parallel pot. The euro engine is untouched; cryptids are a bolt-on gamble.
New resourcesSightings (cheap face-down tokens) and one central Legend card (the Pot) per game. No new currency in the core economy.
New actionsReport a Sighting (pay 1 Specimen, add a token face-down to the Pot) · Fund an Inquiry (peek the Truth, or add weight) · Cry Hoax (force an early resolution).
Sample effectsThe Pot resolves once near end-game by weighted draw. Genuine → biggest contributor gains a windfall of Standing; Hoax → biggest contributor is publicly ruined. A "Sensational Discovery" tactic can slam the resolution early.
Rules weightLight. One shared component, three optional actions.
Best resolverD · the Kraken Pot.
VibeA side bet under a clean euro — purists can opt out, gamblers feed the beast.

MediumB · Top of the Ladder Recommended start

Cryptids are simply the riskiest rarity of the Species trophies — fully inside the naming engine you're already building.

RoleThe Dubious tier of the trophy ladder (§2). Same recipes, same Hall, plus the hidden-truth layer.
New resourcesEvidence tokens (Genuine / Doubt) collected on a creature's dossier as you commit Findings; one shared evidence bag.
New actionsLodge Evidence (spend a Finding → add a token, may peek) · Call for Verification (trigger the weighted draw to settle it) · Publish a Refutation (the existing Hall attack, repurposed to add Doubt or debunk).
Sample effectsTrait synergies extend to cryptids. A cryptid confirmed real grants a strong passive ("your claims can no longer be debunked"). A debunked one still scores Notoriety scraps. The Sensationalist can name a cryptid fast for instant Standing — at dispute risk.
Rules weightMedium. Extends the trophy system; no new lane, no new currency.
Best resolverC · Weight of Evidence (or A · Sealed Dossier for a bluffier table).
VibeThe deepest theme for the least new structure — cryptids feel like the dangerous top shelf of the same shelf.

HeavyC · The Sensation Economy

A whole second strategic path — the Showman beside the Systematist — built on a volatile new currency.

RoleA parallel economy. Alongside sober Findings → Claims, you can chase Rumor → Sensation for faster, riskier glory.
New resourcesRumor (from sightings & the press) refines into Notoriety (spends like Standing but crashes when the Bubble bursts). A shared Bubble clock.
New actionsCourt the Press (Specimens/Findings → Rumor) · Stage a Sensation (Rumor → Notoriety; inflate the Bubble) · Expose a Fraud (pop a rival's bubble; dump their unbanked Notoriety).
Sample effectsA second viable win route. Notoriety buys Standing and tempo, but a burst Bubble wipes whatever is unbanked. The Sensationalist faction becomes a true archetype rather than a spoiler.
Rules weightHeavy. A genuine second economy — risks a fourth currency.
Best resolverE + D (the Bubble bursting is a Schrödinger-style mass resolution at the clock).
VibeMaximum depth and divergent strategies; the biggest balance risk. Echoes the Spice & Ruin bubble treatment.
Caution
The Specimens → Findings → Claims gradient is the spine of the whole design. A fourth currency is the single most dangerous addition here — prototype B first and only graduate to C if play feels one-dimensional.

Medium–HeavyD · Child of the Expedition

Cryptids exist only as products of the exploration lane; the fraud system is the payoff of pushing your luck into the unknown.

RoleCryptids are encountered, never bought. The fraud drama is the reward of the Expedition lane (§5).
New resourcesProvisions (to mount trips) · a wild Discovery deck · Sighting cards that may be real or fanciful.
New actionsMount Expedition · Press On (push your luck — draw again) · Make Camp (bank what you've found) · Chart the Region (improve future odds / fix the deck).
Sample effectsHazards (fever, storm, lost notes) bust a trip and cost what you'd gathered. Deeper pushes surface rarer, wilder creatures. Sightings from the same region pool anonymously — the feed-the-kraken texture, native. Reality is set by the deck's composition plus a final draw.
Rules weightMedium–Heavy. Only sensible if you adopt the Expedition lane.
Best resolverC / D via the deck's composition.
VibeThe wonder up front; cryptids are the reward for daring — and the clearest justification for a new lane.

These are not mutually exclusive in spirit: B is the spine, A is B with the dial turned down, D is B sourced from a new lane, and C is B grown into its own economy. You can ship B and grow toward C or D as playtests demand.

5The new lanes & the five-lane arc

You asked to delve into both the Expedition and the Cabinet — possibly as a five-lane game. Here is each in full, then an honest read on how far to go.

The Expedition — wild / push-your-luck (a new temperature)

Sits above the Field: the "go into the unknown" step before you even own a specimen. You mount an expedition, then push your luck through a wild Discovery deck — each draw yields a Specimen, a rare lead, a Cryptid sighting, or a Hazard that can bust the trip. Bank now, or press deeper.

Why it earns a slot: it adds the one tension the three temperatures lack — you versus nature and variance (PvE), not versus rivals — and it is the natural source of cryptids. When several expeditions probe the same region, their sightings pool anonymously: precisely your "not clear who found it."

The Cabinet — display / legacy / set-collection

A tableau where named species are enshrined for the public and posterity — the "what was it all for" payoff. Arrange your species into coherent sets for escalating end-game prestige and patronage income. A confirmed-real legendary beast becomes a crown jewel; a famous debunked one still scores Notoriety (the museum everyone visits to laugh).

Its weakness, honestly: as a shared band it is cold and solitaire — quiet point-stacking. To give it teeth, make its prestige spots contested: only one player holds "the definitive collection of British Birds," publicly claimable and stealable by a better collection. That turns it into a slow cold-war over legacy (priority again), rather than private points.

The five-lane arc

Stacked top-to-bottom, the five lanes trace the literal life-cycle of a discovery:

The Expeditionwild / luck — encounter · source of cryptids
The Fieldcold / draft — claim
The Studywarm / work — classify
The Hallhot / battle — name & contest · fraud showdowns
The Cabinetcold / display — enshrine · set-collection legacy
sail out → bring home → study → fight to name → display for posterity
My honest read — the two lanes aren't equal
The Expedition clearly earns a full lane: a new temperature, the home of the wonder, the engine of the cryptid drama. The Cabinet is stronger as a scoring overlay (a personal tableau + end-game sets) than as a fifth shared band, because even when contested it adds length and a second cold zone that blurs the clean "three temperatures" thesis. The version I'd build first is four lanes — add the Expedition, keep the Cabinet as an overlay — and promote the Cabinet to a true fifth lane only if playtests say the legacy fight wants its own contested space. Two cautions for any version: don't let the chain become a slow five-step lap, and resist a fourth or fifth currency.

A play vignette — the Kraken resolves

It is the fifth round and the Great Kraken has been swelling in the centre for an age. Vesterlund, the Sensationalist, has quietly fed it four Sightings, certain it is real — she peeked turns ago. Okonkwo, the Systematist, fed two, hedging. Reyes fed nothing but has hoarded a "Prior Publication" tactic.

Vesterlund Calls for Verification to cash in before the Exhibition closes. The evidence bag now holds six Genuine and four Doubt tokens — the table's findings, made odds. The draw comes up Doubt. The Kraken is declared a magnificent hoax. Vesterlund, the largest contributor, is ruined: she loses Standing equal to all four Sightings and her grand Hall lead with it. Okonkwo shrugs off a small loss; Reyes, who never touched it, glides into the Standing lead and the season ends in his favour.

Every element you wanted is in that paragraph: a creature real-or-fake by the luck of the bag, weighted by what the table chose to believe; anonymous feeding; brinkmanship; and a scoop decided as much by who didn't over-commit as by who did.

8What to lock next

Three decisions turn this into a prototype. In rough order:

  1. Confirm the spine. Adopt trophies (§2) as the objective layer — this alone fixes the shallowness, cryptids or not.
  2. Choose an integration model. The low-risk path is B · Top of the Ladder with resolver C · Weight of Evidence; the dramatic path adds the Kraken Pot (D) on top. Both ship without a new currency.
  3. Decide on the Expedition lane. If yes, fold in Model D and let the lane source your cryptids. Keep the Cabinet as a scoring overlay until play asks for more.
Suggested first prototype
Trophies + Model B + resolver C, on the existing three lanes, with ~6 real species per field and ~6 cryptids. Add the Expedition lane and the Kraken Pot only once the trophy race feels good on its own. Smallest change that tests the biggest idea.
